Session 1.1: Prompt Engineering 101 for Recruiters

Your guide to moving from simple questions to expert commands.

The #1 Skill for Working with AI

The goal today is to become an expert instructor for your AI assistant. In our survey, the #1 skill gap was knowing how to get the AI to do what you actually want.

Think of AI as the world's most brilliant junior assistant: it's incredibly fast and knowledgeable, but it has zero context about your job. A vague instruction gets a vague result. An expert instruction gets an expert result.

A Tale of Two Prompts

The "Before" Prompt

This is how most people start. The result is generic, boring, and won't attract top talent because the AI has to guess everything.

write a job description for a software engineer

The "After" Prompt

This is an expert command. It gives the AI a professional brief, not a vague question, leading to a high-quality result.

[ROLE] You are an expert RPO sourcer...
[CONTEXT] We are hiring a "Senior Full-Stack Engineer"...
[TASK] Write a compelling job description...
[FORMAT] Structure the output with sections...

The R.C.T.F.E. Framework

An expert prompt contains five key ingredients. Mastering this structure is the key to getting high-quality results every time.

Role

Who is the AI?

Context

What's the background?

Task

What should it do?

Format

How should it look?

Exemplar

What's a good example?

Session 2.1: AI for Advanced Sourcing

Our Goal: To use AI to move beyond obvious keywords and become true market intelligence experts. We will find better candidates, faster.

Part A: Boolean & Beyond

Stop Brainstorming Keywords. Start Generating Them.

The old way of writing a Boolean string is slow. You try to think of every possible synonym and job title. The new way is to tell the AI what you need and let it do the heavy lifting.

The Evolution of a Search Prompt:

  1. Start with a simple idea: "Software Engineer with Python."
  2. Ask AI to expand it: "Give me a list of 20 alternative job titles and 30 related technical skills."
  3. Ask AI to build it: "Using these titles and skills, build me a complex Boolean search string for LinkedIn."
View the "Boolean Builder" Prompt Example
[ROLE] You are an expert tech sourcer with deep knowledge of LinkedIn Recruiter's search syntax.

[CONTEXT] I am looking for a "Senior Software Engineer" with experience in Python, AWS, and database management. I need to create a comprehensive search string to find candidates in the Budapest area.

[TASK] First, brainstorm a list of 15 alternative job titles and 20 related technical skills for this role. Then, using all of those terms, build me a complex, multi-layered Boolean search string that is optimized for LinkedIn Recruiter.

[FORMAT] Provide the brainstormed lists first, then provide the final Boolean string in a code block.

Part B: AI as Your Research Assistant

Answer Questions in Minutes, Not Hours.

Sourcing isn't just about finding people; it's about understanding the market. AI can be your personal research analyst. Here are three key research prompts:

1. Market Mapping

"List the top 10 biotech companies specializing in gene therapy in the Boston area."

2. Competitor Analysis

"Who are the main competitors for Company X, and what are their typical software engineering job titles?"

3. Rapid CV Analysis

"Summarize this CV and create a table comparing the candidate's experience against these 5 key requirements from the job description."

Session 2.3: Responsible AI & Final Showcase

Our Goal: To cover the most important topic of becoming responsible and ethical AI users, and then to celebrate your progress with a final showcase.

What is AI Bias?

AI models learn from vast amounts of internet data. The problem is that this data reflects the world as it has been, including all of its historical and unconscious biases. If we're not careful, the AI can simply echo and amplify these past biases.

Our job is to be the filter that ensures fairness and equity.

Where Can Bias Hide in Recruiting?

In Job Descriptions

  • Using gendered language (e.g., "rockstar," "ninja," "aggressive salesman").
  • Listing overly specific requirements that might exclude diverse talent pools (e.g., a degree from only one specific university).

In CV Summaries

  • Over-emphasizing traditional career paths and overlooking non-traditional experience.
  • Unintentionally associating certain names with certain skills based on biased training data.

Your critical eye is the most important tool.

Lab Example: Fixing the Prompt

In the lab, we practice identifying and correcting potentially biased AI outputs by rewriting the prompt that created them.

The Biased AI Output:

"We're looking for a digital native and recent graduate who is a true code ninja to join our young, energetic team."

Potential Bias: Ageism ("recent graduate," "young"), gendered language ("ninja"), excludes experienced candidates.

The Corrected, Inclusive Prompt:

Write a job description for a software developer role. The ideal candidate should be proficient with modern coding practices and passionate about collaborative teamwork. The tone should be inclusive, professional, and welcoming to candidates of all ages and experience levels.

The Most Important Principle

You are the "Human in the Loop."

AI is a powerful assistant, but it is not the decision-maker. You are the final editor, the final judge, and the final quality check. Your industry expertise and human judgment are what make this partnership work.

Never Trust, Always Verify.

Session 3.2: Building an Intelligent Knowledge Base (No-Code RAG)

Let's ask a question that was impossible yesterday: What if you could "talk" to your entire team's history of CVs and interview notes? Today, we're going to build the tool that does exactly that.

What is RAG? (The Simple Explanation)

It stands for Retrieval-Augmented Generation, and it sounds complicated, but the concept is simple.

"RAG is the process of giving a brilliant expert (the AI) your private library of documents to read before they answer your question."

This ensures the answers are grounded in your data, not the public internet. It's secure, relevant, and incredibly powerful.

The Power of RAG: Meet Your "CV & Candidate Info Bot"

This is what our final product will be able to do. Imagine asking a chat bot these questions about your team's private CV database:

  • "Which candidates have experience with both Python and AWS?"
  • "Summarize Jane Doe's experience and highlight her top 3 skills."

How Does Automation Actually Work?

APIs

Think of an API as a waiter in a restaurant. It's how different apps talk to each other, taking requests and returning data.

Triggers

This is the "When this happens..." part of the equation. For us, it's "When a new email gets the label 'Candidate Feedback'..."

Actions

This is the "...Then do that" part. For us, it's "...run my script to analyze it and add it to the spreadsheet."

Session 5.1: Becoming an AI Champion

Your journey so far has made you a skilled power user. This session is about the final, most important step: learning how to share that knowledge. You are now the AI leaders for your team. Your role is to empower, guide, and support.

The Mentor Mindset

Coach, Don't Just Tell

The biggest shift you will make is from being the "expert with the answers" to becoming an "enabler of others' success."

Telling

"Here, let me just write the prompt for you."

Result: Solves one problem, but doesn't build skill.

Coaching

"That's a great question. What have you tried so far?"

Result: Builds your colleague's confidence and problem-solving ability.

Your goal is to make yourself redundant. Teach them how to fish.

The "Ask, Don't Tell" Model in Action

Instead of giving the answer, use these guiding questions to help your colleagues find their own solution.

"What's the ideal output you're looking for?"
"What's the most important piece of context the AI might be missing?"
"If you were to give the AI a role, what would it be?"
"What have you tried so far, and what were the results?"

How to Lead a Great Prompt Clinic

When a colleague is stuck, use this simple 5-step framework to guide a group problem-solving session.

1. Set the Stage: Start by saying, "This is a no-judgment zone. Let's solve this together."

2. Understand the Goal: Let the person explain what they want to achieve.

3. Open the Floor: Ask the group, "What ideas does the team have?"

4. Guide, Don't Drive: Help the user refine their own prompt based on the group's feedback.

5. Summarize the Win: End with, "Great! The key change that worked here was adding more context."

Session 7.1: The ROI of AI in Recruiting

A Strategic Workshop for RPO Leadership. Our goal is to build a data-driven, compelling business case for our top-priority project, turning our strategic ideas into funded, measurable projects.

Defining Success

What Does "Good" Look Like? Moving Beyond Anecdotes

Leading Indicators

These are the early signals of progress that show we're on the right track.

  • Recruiter time saved per week
  • Number of qualified candidates sourced via an AI tool

Lagging Indicators

These are the long-term business impact metrics that prove the value of our work.

  • Reduction in average time-to-fill
  • Improvement in 6-month new-hire retention

We need a mix of both to tell the full story of our success.

Calculating ROI: The Two Sides of Value

Hard ROI: The Financial Case

This is the quantifiable, bottom-line impact. It's the number that gets budgets approved.

Formula:

(Time Saved) x (# of People) x (Avg. Salary) = $$$ Saved

Soft ROI: The Strategic Case

This is the harder-to-measure but equally important strategic value that drives long-term success.

  • Improved candidate quality
  • Higher hiring manager satisfaction
  • Enhanced team morale & retention

The Simple Math of Hard ROI

A Conservative Calculation Example

The Project: Automating the summarization of interview feedback.

The Assumption: This saves each of our 20 recruiters an average of 2 hours per week.

The Calculation:

  • 20 recruiters x 2 hours/week = 40 hours saved per week.
  • 40 hours/week x 50 weeks/year = 2,000 hours saved per year.
  • 2,000 hours x [Average Recruiter Hourly Rate] = $X in annual productivity gain.

This is a powerful, conservative number to anchor your business case.
